Formal controller synthesis using evolutionary methods

Context

PhD position at Delft University of Technology, 2015-2020.

Motivation

Control design for modern safety-critical cyber-physical systems still requires significant expert-knowledge, since for general hybrid systems with temporal logic specifications there are no constructive methods. Nevertheless, in recent years multiple approaches have been proposed to automatically synthesize correct-by-construction controllers. However, typically these methods either result in enormous look-up tables, require online optimization, or are highly dependent on expert-knowledge.

Goal

The goal of this project was to propose a novel approach that overcomes these limitations, i.e. to propose a framework for automatic controller synthesis, capable of synthesizing interpretable closed-form controllers for hybrid systems with temporal logic specifications, without a heavy reliance on expert-knowledge.

Approach

We utilized methods showing great similarities to the human design process, namely evolutionary algorithms and counterexample-guided inductive synthesis. Specifically, we used genetic programming (GP), an evolutionary algorithm capable of evolving entire programs. This made it possible to automatically discover the structure of a solution. Moreover, it enabled the synthesis of compact and interpretable closed-form controllers, circumventing the need for look-up tables or online optimization. In combination with GP, we used the concept of counterexample-guided inductive synthesis (CEGIS) to refine candidate solutions based on counterexamples, until the controller is guaranteed to satisfy the desired specification. We have explored two CEGIS-based synthesis frameworks, which differ in the employed verification paradigms, namely utilizing either (co-synthesized) Lyapunov-like functions or reachability analysis. Both frameworks result in correct-by-construction compact closed-form controllers, where the use of expert-knowledge is optional. Both frameworks are capable of synthesizing sampled-data controllers, enabling implementation in embedded hardware with limited memory and computation power, forming a stepping stone towards faster automation.

Responsibilities

The overall project was carried out in a collaboration between Delft University of Technology, Eindhoven University of Technology, and various industrial partners. My responsibilities as a PhD candidate included:

  • Combining several techniques, including genetic programming, reinforcement learning, certificate functions, SMT solvers and reachability analysis, to automatically synthesize correct-by-construction controllers.
  • The implementation of developed algorithms in Mathematica, Matlab and Python.
  • The publication of several conference and journal papers.

Geometric control of an under-actuated balancing robot

Context

Master thesis, in collaboration with Alten Mechatronics, 2014-2015.

Motivation

Ball-balancing robots, or Ballbots, are mobile robots that balance on top of a single ball. The robot in this project had as its primary goal to be an eye-catcher at technical events. As such, the primary goal of this project was to go beyond balancing and use the full range of motion of the robot. While a linear approach is sufficient for balancing, the full range of motion requires a highly nonlinear model, dominated by three-dimensional rotations. However, parameterizations of these rotations, e.g. Euler angles, can result in issues such as a `gimbal lock' or singularities.

Goal

The goal of this project was to develop a geometric control strategy, i.e. free of parameterization of the rotations, enabling the full range of motion of the Ballbot.

Approach

Instead of parameterizing rotations, we directly considered the rotations on their underlying differential manifolds. We derived a new coordinate-free model using screw theory. Based on this model, we designed two geometric controllers and a geometric observer, which utilized rotational errors that were directly defined on their underlying manifolds. Specifically, we designed a computed torque controller and a sliding mode controller, that can track the attitude of the robot on either the special orthogonal group or the 2-sphere. Positional control of the robot was achieved through the use of the relation between the desired linear acceleration and the attitude of the robot. Finally, initial steps were made to implement the controller in the real platform.

Responsibilities

This project was part of an in-house project of Alten Mechatronics to develop a demonstrator at technical events. My tasks as an MSc student included:

  • The derivation of a model, based on screw theory.
  • The design of a geometric computed-torque controller, sliding mode controller, and observer.
  • Modeling, design and simulation in Matlab.
  • Implementation and debugging in C++.
